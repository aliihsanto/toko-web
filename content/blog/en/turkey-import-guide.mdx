## Why Import from Turkey?

Turkey has rapidly emerged as one of the world's most compelling sourcing destinations, and the numbers tell the story clearly. In 2025, Turkey's total merchandise exports reached $267 billion, and the government has set an ambitious target of $280 billion for 2026. The country now exports to more than 200 countries and territories, and it ranks among the top 20 trading nations on the planet.

But the appeal of importing from Turkey goes far beyond raw export volumes. What makes Turkey genuinely unique is the combination of geographic proximity to Europe, Middle Eastern, and African markets, membership in the EU Customs Union, a young and skilled workforce of over 85 million people, and an industrial base that spans virtually every product category. Whether you are sourcing textiles, food products, construction materials, automotive components, or consumer electronics, Turkey has established suppliers capable of meeting demanding international quality standards.

> Turkey exports to more than 200 countries, has over 30 active free trade agreements, and is on track to surpass $280 billion in annual exports by the end of 2026. Delivery to European markets takes just 3-7 days by road, compared to 30-45 days from the Far East by sea.

### 7 Key Advantages of Importing from Turkey

1. **EU Customs Union membership**: Since 1996, industrial products traded between Turkey and the EU face zero customs duties. This is a massive competitive advantage over suppliers in China, India, Vietnam, or Bangladesh.
2. **Geographic proximity**: Road freight reaches Central Europe in 3-7 days. Sea freight to Mediterranean ports takes 5-10 days. Air freight arrives within 1-3 days.
3. **Competitive production costs**: Labour costs are 50-70% lower than Western Europe while quality standards remain aligned with EU norms. Factory rents and energy costs add further savings.
4. **Quality and compliance**: Turkish manufacturers routinely hold CE, ISO 9001, OEKO-TEX, GOTS, HACCP, and other international certifications. Regulatory alignment with the EU means products are ready for European shelves.
5. **Flexibility and low MOQs**: Unlike Far Eastern suppliers that often require 3,000-10,000 piece minimums, many Turkish manufacturers accept orders as low as 100-500 pieces, making them ideal for small and mid-sized businesses.
6. **Sector diversity**: Turkey's industrial base covers textiles, automotive, food and agriculture, construction materials, machinery, chemicals, furniture, and more. This breadth means you can consolidate multiple product categories with suppliers in a single country.
7. **Young, skilled workforce**: With a median age of 33 and over 200 universities producing 900,000 graduates per year (including 100,000 engineers), Turkey has the human capital to support sophisticated manufacturing operations.

## Step-by-Step Import Process

Importing from Turkey follows a structured process. Each step below is critical to minimising risk, controlling costs, and ensuring a smooth supply chain. Skipping or rushing any stage can lead to costly problems down the line.

### Step 1: Market Research and Supplier Identification

Before anything else, you need to identify the right suppliers for your product. Turkey has over 80,000 registered exporter companies, so the challenge is not finding a supplier but rather finding the *right* one.

**Recommended supplier research channels:**

- **Turkish Exporters Assembly (TIM)**: Maintains a database of all registered exporters, searchable by product category and region
- **Istanbul Chamber of Commerce and regional chambers**: Provide verified company lists and trade statistics
- **International trade fairs**: Istanbul hosts world-class fairs such as Texworld, IDEF, WorldFood Istanbul, Unicera (ceramics), and Automechanika. Attending these fairs allows face-to-face meetings with dozens of potential suppliers in a single trip.
- **B2B platforms**: Turkish suppliers are well represented on Alibaba, TradeKey, Kompass, and Turkishexporter.net
- **Professional sourcing companies**: Working with a company like [Toko Trading](/en/services) is the most efficient route, as we conduct on-site factory visits, verify production capacity, check financial stability, and assess quality systems before presenting you with a shortlist of vetted suppliers

**Supplier evaluation criteria to prioritise:**

- Annual production capacity and current utilisation rates
- Quality management certifications (ISO 9001, sector-specific standards)
- Export experience and existing international client portfolio
- Financial stability (commercial registry checks, credit references)
- Communication quality, responsiveness, and English language proficiency
- Willingness to accept your target MOQ and sampling requirements

### Step 2: Sample Request and Quality Assessment

Once you have identified 3-5 potential suppliers, the next step is to request product samples. Sampling is the single most effective way to evaluate quality before committing to a production order.

**What to evaluate in samples:**

- Material quality, weight, and feel compared to your technical specifications
- Colour accuracy against your Pantone references or colour swatches
- Workmanship quality (stitching, finishing, assembly, surface treatment)
- Packaging and labelling compliance with your target market regulations (CE marking, OEKO-TEX labels, care instructions, barcodes)
- Dimensional accuracy against your measurement charts
- Performance testing results (wash testing, abrasion, tensile strength, colourfastness) from independent laboratories where applicable

**Typical sampling timeline:**

| Stage | Duration | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Initial request and tech pack submission | 1-3 days | You send technical files, the supplier confirms feasibility |
| Proto sample production | 7-14 days | First physical sample based on your specifications |
| Sample revisions | 5-10 days per round | Adjustments based on your feedback |
| Pre-production sample (PPS) | 5-7 days | Final sample made with actual production materials |
| Approval and contract signing | 1-3 days | Written confirmation and order placement |

**Cost**: Most suppliers charge $50-200 per sample. Many will deduct or refund sample costs once a production order is confirmed.

### Step 3: Price Negotiation and Incoterms Selection

After sample approval, price negotiations begin. Understanding **Incoterms 2020** is essential at this stage, because the Incoterms you choose directly determine how costs and risks are divided between you and the supplier.

**Most commonly used Incoterms in Turkish trade:**

| Incoterm | Full Name | Seller Covers | Buyer Covers | Best For |
|----------|-----------|---------------|--------------|----------|
| **EXW** | Ex Works | Goods ready at factory gate | All transport, insurance, customs | Experienced importers with their own logistics |
| **FOB** | Free On Board | Costs until loaded onto vessel | Freight, insurance from port of origin | Buyers who have freight forwarder relationships |
| **CIF** | Cost, Insurance & Freight | Costs, insurance, and freight to destination port | Customs clearance and inland delivery at destination | Mid-experience importers seeking simplicity |
| **DDP** | Delivered Duty Paid | Everything including destination customs duties | Nothing beyond receiving the goods | First-time importers who want turnkey delivery |

**Price negotiation strategies:**

- Always obtain quotes from at least 3 suppliers to establish a market benchmark
- The first quoted price typically has a 10-20% negotiation margin
- Ask for tiered pricing: what is the unit price at 500, 1,000, 5,000, and 10,000 pieces?
- Consider payment terms as part of the overall price discussion (e.g., accepting 100% T/T prepayment may unlock a 3-5% discount)
- Frame the conversation around a long-term partnership rather than a one-off order -- suppliers offer better terms to committed buyers
- Negotiate in USD or EUR to avoid Turkish Lira volatility risk

### Step 4: Payment Methods and Security

Payment security is a critical concern in international trade, especially when working with a new supplier for the first time. Here are the four main payment methods used in Turkish imports, ranked from most secure to least secure for the buyer:

**1. Letter of Credit (L/C)**

The gold standard for first-time transactions. Your bank guarantees payment to the supplier's bank upon presentation of compliant shipping documents. This protects both parties: the seller is assured of payment, and the buyer is assured that goods have been shipped as documented.

- **Cost**: 1-3% of the transaction value (bank charges)
- **Processing time**: 5-10 business days to open
- **Best for**: First orders with new suppliers, high-value transactions
- **Watch out for**: Ensure document requirements in the L/C are realistic and achievable by the supplier

**2. Documents Against Payment (D/P)**

The supplier ships goods and sends shipping documents to their bank, which forwards them to your bank. You receive the documents (and thus control of the goods) only upon payment. It is less expensive than an L/C but offers moderate security.

- **Cost**: Bank fees of $100-300 per transaction
- **Best for**: Established relationships where full L/C is not warranted

**3. Telegraphic Transfer (T/T)**

The most common method in practice. Typically structured as 30% advance payment upon order confirmation and 70% balance payment against copy of bill of lading before original documents are released.

- **Cost**: Wire transfer fees of $25-50 per transaction
- **Best for**: Ongoing relationships with trusted suppliers
- **Risk management**: Never pay 100% upfront to a new supplier. The 30/70 split protects both parties.

**4. Open Account**

The supplier ships goods and invoices you with payment terms (typically 30, 60, or 90 days). This carries the highest risk for the supplier and is typically reserved for long-standing partnerships.

- **Best for**: Established, multi-year relationships with proven track records

### Step 5: Production Monitoring and Quality Control

Once the order is confirmed and advance payment made, production begins. Close monitoring during production is essential to catch problems early, when they are cheapest to fix.

**Typical production timelines by product type:**

- **Standard/stock products**: 15-25 days
- **Customised products with ready materials**: 25-35 days
- **Fully custom products including material sourcing**: 35-60 days

**Recommended 3-stage quality control process:**

1. **Initial Production Inspection (IPI)**: Conducted when the first 10-15% of production is complete. Verifies that raw materials, colours, and initial output conform to the approved sample.
2. **During Production Inspection (DPI)**: Conducted when 40-60% of production is complete. Uses AQL 2.5 sampling to check for systematic defects. This is the last chance to make corrections without delaying delivery.
3. **Pre-Shipment Inspection (PSI)**: Conducted when 100% of production is complete and packed. Full AQL 2.5 inspection covering workmanship, measurements, colour, labelling, packaging, and carton drop testing. The shipment should not be released until this inspection is passed.

### Step 6: Shipping and Logistics

Turkey offers multiple transport modes to suit different volumes, urgencies, and budgets:

| Mode | Transit Time | Cost Level | Best For |
|------|-------------|------------|----------|
| **Sea freight (FCL)** | 8-25 days | Most economical | Full container loads (20ft or 40ft) |
| **Sea freight (LCL)** | 12-30 days | Economical | Smaller shipments consolidated with others |
| **Road freight** | 3-7 days | Moderate | European destinations, time-sensitive orders |
| **Air freight** | 1-3 days | Highest | Urgent shipments, high-value/low-weight goods |
| **Express courier** | 2-5 days | High | Samples, documents, small parcels |
| **Multimodal (sea + road)** | 10-18 days | Optimised | Cost-effective combination routes |

### Step 7: Customs Clearance and Delivery

The final step involves clearing goods through your country's customs authority. Having all documents prepared correctly before the shipment arrives is essential to avoid storage charges and delays. Work closely with your customs broker to ensure HS codes, values, and certificates are correct.

## Required Documents: Complete Checklist

Missing or incorrect documentation is the number one cause of delays at customs. Here is the complete checklist of documents you will need when importing from Turkey.

### Mandatory Documents

- **Commercial Invoice**: The primary trade document. Must show seller and buyer details, product description, quantity, unit price, total value, Incoterms, currency, and payment terms. Ensure consistency with all other documents.
- **Bill of Lading (B/L) or Air Waybill (AWB)**: Issued by the carrier confirming receipt of goods for shipment. The B/L is a document of title for sea freight; the AWB serves the same function for air freight but is non-negotiable.
- **Packing List**: Itemised list showing the contents, net and gross weight, dimensions, and marks/numbers of each package or carton. Must match the commercial invoice exactly.
- **Certificate of Origin**: Official document certifying the goods originate in Turkey. Issued and stamped by the local chamber of commerce.
- **ATR Movement Certificate**: The single most important document for Turkey-EU trade. Proves that industrial goods qualify for zero customs duty under the EU Customs Union. Without this certificate, you will pay the full MFN duty rate.
- **EUR.1 Movement Certificate**: Required for preferential tariff treatment when exporting to countries that have a Free Trade Agreement with Turkey (e.g., EFTA countries, South Korea, Morocco, Chile).

### Additional Documents (Product/Situation Dependent)

- **Insurance Certificate**: Required for CIF and CIP Incoterms
- **Health Certificate**: Mandatory for food and animal products
- **Phytosanitary Certificate**: Required for plant and agricultural products
- **Conformity Certificate**: For products subject to technical regulations in the destination country
- **Laboratory/Analysis Report**: Chemical composition or performance test results
- **Surveillance Certificate**: Required for certain regulated products (textiles, steel, ceramics) in specific markets
- **Fumigation Certificate**: For wood packaging materials (ISPM 15 compliance)

## Incoterms Guide for Turkish Trade

Choosing the right Incoterms is one of the most impactful decisions in your import process. The wrong choice can result in unexpected costs, unclear responsibilities, and insurance gaps. Here is a quick decision framework:

- **You are new to importing?** Start with **CIF** or **DDP**. The supplier handles most logistics, and you receive goods at your port or door.
- **You have your own freight forwarder?** Use **FOB**. You control and optimise the freight leg.
- **You want maximum control?** Use **EXW**, but be prepared to handle all transport, insurance, and customs formalities from the supplier's factory gate.
- **You want zero hassle?** Use **DDP**. The supplier delivers to your warehouse with all duties paid. You pay a premium for this convenience.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### 1. Insufficient Supplier Research
**Mistake**: Choosing the first supplier that sends a competitive quote without verifying their credentials.
**Solution**: Always evaluate at least 3-5 suppliers. Conduct factory visits (personally or through a sourcing agent). Check trade references, financial standing, and certification validity.

### 2. Skipping Pre-Shipment Quality Control
**Mistake**: Assuming that approved samples guarantee consistent bulk production quality.
**Solution**: Always conduct pre-shipment AQL inspection, either through your own QC team or an independent inspection company. The cost of a $200-400 inspection is negligible compared to the cost of receiving defective goods.

### 3. Incorrect HS Code Classification
**Mistake**: Using an incorrect or imprecise Harmonized System code on customs declarations.
**Solution**: Work with a qualified customs broker to determine the correct 6-digit (international) and extended (national) HS code. Consider applying for a Binding Tariff Information if you are importing a novel product.

### 4. Ignoring Currency Risk
**Mistake**: Failing to hedge against exchange rate fluctuations between order placement and payment.
**Solution**: Negotiate contracts in USD or EUR. For large transactions, consider forward contracts or currency options through your bank. The Turkish Lira has historically been volatile, so always denominate in a stable currency.

### 5. Incomplete or Incorrect Documentation
**Mistake**: Arriving at customs with missing ATR certificates, mismatched invoice values, or incomplete packing lists.
**Solution**: Create a document checklist and verify every item before shipment. A missing ATR certificate means losing your zero-duty privilege under the EU Customs Union.

### 6. Underestimating Lead Times
**Mistake**: Assuming production and shipping will happen on the fastest possible timeline.
**Solution**: Always build a buffer of 1-2 weeks into your delivery schedule. Account for potential fabric delays, public holidays (especially Ramadan and national holidays), and shipping disruptions.

### 7. Poor Communication Practices
**Mistake**: Relying solely on email and assuming the supplier fully understands your requirements.
**Solution**: Use video calls for technical discussions. Confirm all agreements in writing. Send detailed technical files with visual references. Appoint a single point of contact on each side to avoid confusion.

## Sector-Specific Tips

### Textiles and Apparel
Istanbul, Bursa, Denizli, and Gaziantep are the primary production hubs. MOQs start at 200-500 pieces for garments, 500 kg for home textiles. Turkey excels in fast fashion turnaround (2-4 weeks), organic cotton, and sustainable production. See our detailed [textile sourcing guide](/en/blog/textile-sourcing-guide) for more.

### Food and Agricultural Products
Turkey is the world's largest producer of hazelnuts (70% of global output), dried apricots (80%), and figs. It is also a leading exporter of olive oil, pulses, confectionery, and processed foods. Health certificates, phytosanitary certificates, and proper labelling are mandatory.

### Construction Materials
Ceramics, marble, cement, steel, and glass are Turkey's construction material strengths. Sea freight is the standard transport mode due to weight and volume. Turkey is Europe's second-largest ceramics producer and the world's top marble exporter.

### Machinery and Industrial Equipment
Turkey's machinery sector exports $25 billion annually, covering textile machinery, food processing equipment, agricultural machinery, and plastic processing equipment. After-sales support and spare parts availability should be verified during supplier selection.
