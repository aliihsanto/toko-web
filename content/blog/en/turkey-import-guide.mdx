## Why Import from Turkey?

Turkey has established itself as one of the most dynamic sourcing destinations in the global supply chain. Positioned at the crossroads of Europe and Asia, the country offers a unique combination of competitive manufacturing costs, high quality standards, and strategic proximity to major consumer markets.

With its EU Customs Union membership eliminating duties on industrial goods bound for Europe, Turkey provides a compelling alternative to Far East sourcing for businesses seeking shorter lead times and greater supply chain agility.

> Turkey exports to over 180 countries with an annual trade volume exceeding $250 billion, making it one of the world's top 25 exporting nations and a critical hub in global supply chains.

## The Import Process: Step by Step

Successfully importing from Turkey requires a structured approach. Here is a proven six-step framework:

### 1. Market Research and Supplier Identification

The foundation of any successful import operation is finding the right supplier. Turkey's industrial landscape varies significantly by region -- Istanbul leads in fashion and services, Bursa excels in automotive, Denizli dominates home textiles, and Gaziantep is a carpet manufacturing powerhouse.

Working with a professional sourcing company like Toko Trading can significantly accelerate this process. Local experts conduct factory visits, verify production capabilities, and assess supplier reliability before you commit to any business relationship.

### 2. Sample Request and Quality Assessment

Once potential suppliers are identified, request product samples. Key evaluation criteria include:

- Product quality and consistency with specifications
- Packaging and labeling compliance with your market's regulations
- Certification documentation (CE marking, OEKO-TEX, ISO, etc.)
- Minimum order quantities (MOQ) and flexibility

### 3. Price Negotiation and Order Confirmation

With samples approved, negotiations begin. Understanding Incoterms is essential:

1. **FOB (Free on Board)**: Seller covers costs until goods are loaded on the vessel -- the most common term for Turkish exports
2. **CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight)**: Seller covers freight and insurance to the destination port
3. **EXW (Ex Works)**: Buyer assumes all costs from the factory gate
4. **DDP (Delivered Duty Paid)**: Seller handles everything including customs clearance at destination

### 4. Production Monitoring

After order confirmation, production timelines typically range from 15 to 45 days depending on the product complexity. Key milestones to track:

- Raw material procurement
- Production commencement
- In-line quality inspection
- Pre-shipment final inspection
- Packaging and loading

### 5. Shipping and Logistics

Turkey offers multiple shipping options to suit different budgets and timelines:

- **Sea freight**: 10-25 days, most cost-effective for large volumes
- **Road freight**: 3-7 days to European destinations
- **Air freight**: 1-3 days for urgent or high-value shipments
- **Rail freight**: Growing option for Central Asian destinations

### 6. Customs Clearance and Delivery

Upon arrival at the destination country, goods must clear customs. This involves submitting the customs declaration, paying applicable duties and taxes, and obtaining release clearance.

## Required Documents

When importing from Turkey, you will typically need the following documents:

- **Commercial Invoice**: Official document showing the value and terms of trade
- **Bill of Lading (B/L)**: Transport document for sea freight
- **Packing List**: Detailed breakdown of packaged goods
- **Certificate of Origin**: Proves goods are manufactured in Turkey
- **ATR Movement Certificate**: Enables duty-free access for industrial goods within the EU Customs Union
- **EUR.1 Certificate**: For countries with Free Trade Agreements with Turkey
- **Insurance Certificate**: Required for CIF/CIP shipments

## Common Challenges and How to Overcome Them

International trade always involves challenges. Here are the most common ones when importing from Turkey:

1. **Language barriers**: While many Turkish manufacturers speak English, technical discussions benefit from local intermediaries
2. **Quality control**: Always conduct pre-shipment inspections through independent third parties
3. **Payment security**: Use Letters of Credit (L/C) for initial orders; transition to T/T (wire transfer) as trust builds
4. **Tariff classification**: Incorrect HS code assignment can lead to unexpected duties or penalties
5. **Currency fluctuations**: The Turkish Lira's volatility can affect pricing -- negotiate in USD or EUR for stability
